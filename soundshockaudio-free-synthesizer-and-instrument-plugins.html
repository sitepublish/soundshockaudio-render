MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is a technical standard describing a communications protocol, digital interface, and electrical connectors that connect a wide variety of electronic musical instruments, computers, and related audio devices. Developed in the early 1980s through collaboration between manufacturers including Roland, Yamaha, Korg, and Sequential Circuits, MIDI was first demonstrated publicly in January 1983. The protocol transmits event messages that specify musical notation, pitch, velocity, vibrato, panning, and clock signals to synchronize tempo. MIDI does not transmit audio signals; rather, it communicates performance data that receiving devices interpret to generate sound. The standard operates at 31.25 kbit/s and uses 5-pin DIN connectors in its traditional hardware form. MIDI 2.0, ratified in 2020, introduced higher resolution controllers, per-note articulation, bidirectional communication, and backward compatibility with MIDI 1.0 devices.

Music theory is the study of the practices and possibilities of music, encompassing the analysis of rhythm, harmony, melody, form, texture, and timbre. Western music theory is built upon a twelve-tone equal temperament system where the octave is divided into twelve equal semitones. Scales, which are ordered sequences of notes following specific interval patterns, provide the tonal framework for melodies and harmonies. The major scale follows a whole-whole-half-whole-whole-whole-half step pattern, while the natural minor scale follows whole-half-whole-whole-half-whole-whole. Chords are formed by stacking intervals of thirds, with triads (three notes) and seventh chords (four notes) serving as the harmonic foundation of most Western music. Chord progressions create harmonic movement and tension-resolution patterns that drive musical narrative. While formal music theory knowledge is not required for music production, understanding basic concepts of scales, chords, and rhythm significantly accelerates the creative process and enables more intentional compositional decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I install a VST plugin?
Most VST plugins come with an installer that places the plugin files in your system's VST folder. On Windows, the default location is typically C:\Program Files\VSTPlugins or C:\Program Files\Common Files\VST3. On macOS, plugins go in /Library/Audio/Plug-Ins/VST or VST3. After installation, rescan your plugins in your DAW.
What equipment do I need for a home studio?
At minimum, you need a computer, a DAW, headphones, and an audio interface. As you progress, add studio monitors, a microphone, acoustic treatment, and a MIDI controller. SoundShockAudio recommends starting with software tools first and gradually investing in hardware as your skills and needs develop.
What is an audio interface?
An audio interface is an external device that converts analog audio signals to digital data for your computer and vice versa. It provides high-quality microphone preamps, instrument inputs, headphone outputs, and monitor outputs with lower latency than built-in sound cards. Popular affordable interfaces include the Focusrite Scarlett series and PreSonus AudioBox.
What is acoustic treatment and why does it matter?
Acoustic treatment uses absorptive and diffusive materials to control sound reflections in a room, ensuring you hear an accurate representation of your audio. Without treatment, room reflections cause frequency buildup and cancellation that mislead your mixing decisions. Bass traps in corners and absorption panels at reflection points dramatically improve monitoring accuracy.
