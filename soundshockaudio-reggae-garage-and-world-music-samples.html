Reference

Electronic music history traces back to the late 19th century with inventions like the Telharmonium (1897) and gained momentum through the 20th century. The Theremin (1920) and Ondes Martenot (1928) were among the first electronic instruments to reach public audiences. Musique concrete, developed by Pierre Schaeffer in the 1940s, pioneered the manipulation of recorded sounds as compositional material. The development of the Moog synthesizer in the 1960s brought electronic sound creation to popular music, with Wendy Carlos's "Switched-On Bach" (1968) demonstrating its musical potential. The 1970s and 1980s saw the rise of Kraftwerk, Giorgio Moroder, and Yellow Magic Orchestra, who established electronic music as a mainstream force. The advent of MIDI (1983), affordable digital synthesizers, and drum machines like the Roland TR-808 and TR-909 catalyzed the emergence of house music in Chicago, techno in Detroit, and acid house in the UK, genres that spawned the global electronic dance music culture.

Audio frequency refers to the rate of vibration of sound waves, measured in hertz (Hz), that determines the perceived pitch of a sound. The human audible range spans approximately 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz), though this range diminishes with age, particularly at higher frequencies. In music production, the frequency spectrum is commonly divided into ranges: sub-bass (20-60 Hz), bass (60-250 Hz), low midrange (250-500 Hz), midrange (500 Hz-2 kHz), upper midrange (2-4 kHz), presence (4-6 kHz), and brilliance (6-20 kHz). Each musical instrument occupies characteristic frequency ranges, and understanding these ranges is essential for effective mixing and equalization. The fundamental frequency of a note determines its pitch, while the relative amplitudes of its harmonics (integer multiples of the fundamental) define its timbre or tonal quality, allowing listeners to distinguish between instruments playing the same note.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is automation in a DAW?
Automation allows you to program changes to any parameter over time, such as volume fades, panning movements, filter sweeps, or effect sends. Drawing or recording automation adds dynamic movement and expression to static mixes. Strategic automation is what separates professional-sounding productions from flat, lifeless ones.
How do I remove background noise from recordings?
Use a noise gate to silence audio below a set threshold, cutting out low-level background noise between phrases. For more thorough removal, spectral editing tools like iZotope RX or free alternatives like Audacity's noise reduction can profile and subtract consistent background noise. Prevention through good recording practices is always more effective than post-processing.
What is vocal processing?
Vocal processing involves applying a chain of effects to shape the tone, dynamics, and character of a vocal recording. A typical chain includes pitch correction, EQ, compression, de-essing, and reverb or delay. SoundShockAudio features many free vocal processing plugins to help you get started with professional vocal chains.
What is the difference between VST2 and VST3?
VST3 is the newer format that offers improvements over VST2, including better CPU efficiency, resizable interfaces, and support for multiple audio buses. VST3 plugins only process audio when there is input signal, saving CPU resources. Many DAWs now prefer or require VST3 format.
